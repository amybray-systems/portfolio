Why Existing Tools Don't Fix This

Every company I worked at had HR portals, wikis, FAQs, onboarding docs. None of them solved this problem. Why?

Because to use them, you have to know what you're looking for.

If you don't know you're "W-2 contingent through an EOR," you can't search for "EOR benefits." You just know you work at UnBungle and you want to know about your health insurance. Enter: Aunikah

So I built a chatbot that meets people where they are. You don't need to know the terminology. You just tell it what you want, and it figures out the rest.

The breakthrough moment was adding the "I'm not sure" option.

The Team Member vs. Employee Thing

Another thing needed ironed out: terminology matters SO MUCH.

I kept to referring everyone "employees" in the first draft. Then I realized:

  • 1099 contractors aren't employees (that's literally the whole point)
  • W-2 contingent workers ARE employees... but of the EOR, not the client or the marketplace
  • "Worker" is technically accurate but feels kind of cold and transactional
  • "Talent" is cute but overloaded in marketplace branding
  • "Employee" is legally wrong half the time and confusing the other half

So inside the chatbot itself, I use "team member" when I‚Äôm talking about people as humans on a team, and I switch to "employee," "contractor," or "W-2 contingent" only when we‚Äôre in legal/benefits territory and it actually matters.

The Manager Side (aka The Database Trick)

One of my favorite features is manager mode. Managers often don't remember which of their contingent workers is W-2 through which EOR vs. 1099. Why would they? It's not their job to track that.

But it is their job to answer basic questions like ‚ÄúCan I approve this PTO?‚Äù or ‚ÄúWhere should I send them for a benefits question?‚Äù Without context, they end up guessing or forwarding people around.

So in Aunikah, managers just pick the team member's name from the list. The system "knows" (from the database) whether that person is W-2 or 1099, which EOR employs them, and which client they're at.

Then when the manager asks "What are Sarah's benefits?" Aunikah responds with Sarah-specific info:

"Sarah Chen is a W-2 Contingent Worker employed by Employee Stand and working at UnBungle. Benefits include health/dental/vision through Employee Stand's plan, 401(k) with 4% match, and 15 days of PTO..."

No more "uh, which one is she again?" The system handles it. The manager just has to care and ask.

In production, this would be an API call to your HRIS. In the demo, it's just a JavaScript object. But the concept is the same: make it easy for managers to support their teams without having to memorize everyone's employment status or vendor setup.

What I Learned (The Messy Bits)

1. Scope creep is real and I almost went global

At one point I was like "ooh, I should add international classifications! W-8BEN forms! VAT!"

Then I caught myself. This is a DEMO. US-only is fine. The point is to show I understand the problem, not to solve every global employment classification scenario.

Sometimes done is better than perfect. (I'm still learning this one.)

2. The paystub trick is major in solving the classification question

When people are confused about their classification, asking them to look at their paystub or how they are paid is magic. It's physical proof they can hold in their hands (or open on their phone). No ambiguity. That company name is the answer.

For marketplaces, it‚Äôs also a gentle way to say, ‚ÄúI know you interacted with us a lot, but legally, this is who employs you‚Äù without making them feel silly.

3. Personalization makes it feel real

When Aunikah says "Benefits for Sarah Chen are handled by Employee Stand" instead of "Benefits are handled by their EOR," it feels custom-built. Like the system actually knows them.

Same for managers: ‚ÄúThis is a 1099 contractor; don‚Äôt promise PTO‚Äù lands differently than a generic HR blurb.

That's what makes demos compelling‚Äîit feels like it could actually work in production.
