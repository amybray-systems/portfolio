The Tech Stack (and Why)

Python 3.11 Streamlit Pandas NumPy Plotly OpenPyXL

Why Python? I needed something fast to prototype and powerful enough to handle real operations data. Python + Pandas is the gold standard for data transformation.

Why Streamlit? I wanted to ship something usable, not just a Jupyter notebook. Streamlit let me go from prototype to live demo in hours, not weeks. Plus, it handles file uploads, charts, and dark mode out of the box.

Why Plotly? Because Excel charts in 2025 are sad. Plotly gives me interactive, presentation-ready visualizations that actually help people understand the data.

Key Design Decisions

1. Real-time, not batch

Every upload triggers immediate processing. No "submit and wait." You see results as soon as the data loads. This mirrors how I actually wanted to work‚Äîupload, verify, export. Done.

2. Data validation first

Bad data in = bad insights out. The pipeline automatically checks for:

  • Null values and their impact
  • Duplicate entries
  • Missing required columns
  • Suspicious outliers in hours/rates

Catch issues before they become problems downstream.

3. Dark mode isn't optional

Financial analysts work late. I work late. Blinding white interfaces at 9 PM are torture. Dark mode is a localStorage-persisted toggle that works across both tools.

4. Export everything

Great insights trapped in a tool are useless. Every analysis can be exported as Excel with the click of a button. Because sometimes finance just wants the spreadsheet.

What I Learned

1. Build for the pain, not the theory.
I didn't build a "universal data platform." I built two specific tools for two specific pain points I lived with every week. That specificity is why they actually work.

2. Fast iteration beats perfect planning.
Streamlit let me prototype the data pipeline in a weekend. I showed it to former colleagues, got feedback, and iterated. Weeks later, it was battle-tested.

3. Real data is messier than you think.
Test data is clean. Real vendor reports have merged cells, inconsistent date formats, and random blank rows. Building for the mess made the tool robust.

4. Visual feedback is mandatory.
Users trust systems that show them what's happening. Progress bars, validation summaries, preview tables‚Äîthese aren't "nice to have," they're essential.
